Definition: Quotient Group

Definition: Quotient Group

A quotient group (also called a factor group) is a group formed by partitioning a group into cosets of a normal subgroup.

Formal Definition

Let \(G\) be a group and \(N \triangleleft G\) be a normal subgroup. The quotient group of \(G\) by \(N\), denoted \(G/N\) (read ‚Äú\(G\) modulo \(N\)‚Äù), is the set of all left cosets of \(N\) in \(G\):

\[G/N = \{gN : g \in G\}\]

where \(gN = \{gn : n \in N\}\) is the left coset of \(N\) containing \(g\).

Group Structure

The quotient \(G/N\) becomes a group under the operation:

\[(gN) \cdot (hN) = (gh)N\]

This operation is well-defined precisely because \(N\) is normal in \(G\).

Verification of Group Axioms

  1. Closure: For any \(gN, hN \in G/N\), we have \((gN)(hN) = (gh)N \in G/N\)

  2. Associativity: Inherited from the associativity in \(G\): \[((gN)(hN))(kN) = (gh)N \cdot kN = ((gh)k)N = (g(hk))N = gN \cdot (hk)N = (gN)((hN)(kN))\]

  3. Identity: The coset \(eN = N\) serves as the identity element: \[(gN) \cdot N = (gN) \cdot (eN) = (ge)N = gN\]

  4. Inverses: For each \(gN \in G/N\), the inverse is \((g^{-1})N\): \[(gN) \cdot (g^{-1}N) = (gg^{-1})N = eN = N\]

Fundamental Properties

Order of Quotient Group

If \(G\) is finite, then: \[|G/N| = \frac{|G|}{|N|} = [G:N]\]

This is the index of \(N\) in \(G\).

The Natural Homomorphism

The map \(\pi: G \to G/N\) defined by \(\pi(g) = gN\) is a surjective group homomorphism called the natural homomorphism or canonical projection. Its kernel is exactly \(N\): \[\ker(\pi) = N\]

Inheritance of Properties

  1. If \(G\) is abelian, then \(G/N\) is abelian
  2. If \(G\) is cyclic, then \(G/N\) is cyclic
  3. If \(G\) is finitely generated, then \(G/N\) is finitely generated

Examples

1. Integers Modulo n

The quotient group \(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}\) consists of the residue classes modulo \(n\): \[\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} = \{0 + n\mathbb{Z}, 1 + n\mathbb{Z}, ..., (n-1) + n\mathbb{Z}\}\]

This is the cyclic group of order \(n\), often denoted \(\mathbb{Z}_n\) or \(C_n\).

2. The Circle Group

The quotient \(\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}\) can be identified with the circle group \(S^1\): - Elements are cosets \(x + \mathbb{Z}\) for \(x \in \mathbb{R}\) - Two reals \(x, y\) are in the same coset iff \(x - y \in \mathbb{Z}\)

3. Quotient by the Center

For any group \(G\), the quotient \(G/Z(G)\) by its center measures how ‚Äúnon-abelian‚Äù the group is: - If \(G/Z(G)\) is cyclic, then \(G\) is abelian - If \(|G/Z(G)| = 1\), then \(G = Z(G)\), so \(G\) is abelian

4. Simple Groups

A group \(G\) is simple if its only normal subgroups are \(\{e\}\) and \(G\) itself. For simple groups, the only quotient groups are: - \(G/\{e\} \cong G\) - \(G/G \cong \{e\}\)

Geometric Interpretation

Quotient groups can be visualized as: - Collapsing the normal subgroup \(N\) to a single point (the identity) - Identifying elements that differ by an element of \(N\) - Creating a ‚Äúsimpler‚Äù group by ‚Äúmodding out‚Äù the structure of \(N\)

Applications

First Isomorphism Theorem

The First Isomorphism Theorem states that if \(\phi: G \to H\) is a group homomorphism, then: \[G/\ker(\phi) \cong \text{Im}(\phi)\]

Exact Sequences

In the short exact sequence: \[1 \to N \to G \to G/N \to 1\]

The quotient group \(G/N\) captures the ‚Äúcomplement‚Äù of \(N\) in \(G\).

Parallel Concepts in Other Algebraic Structures

The concept of quotient groups generalizes to other algebraic structures:

  • Quotient Rings: Just as normal subgroups allow us to form quotient groups, Ideals allow us to form quotient rings. For example, \(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}\) can be viewed both as a quotient group (of the additive group \(\mathbb{Z}\)) and as a quotient ring.
  • Quotient Vector Spaces: Vector spaces can be quotiented by subspaces
  • Quotient Modules: The most general case, encompassing both groups and vector spaces

This pattern of ‚Äúmodding out‚Äù by a suitable substructure is fundamental throughout algebra.
