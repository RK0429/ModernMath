Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is the central theorem of calculus that establishes the profound connection between differentiation and integration, showing that these two operations are essentially inverses of one another.

Statement

The theorem consists of two closely related parts:

Part I (First Fundamental Theorem)

Let \(f\) be a continuous function on \([a,b]\). Define the function \(F\) on \([a,b]\) by:

\[F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt\]

Then \(F\) is differentiable on \((a,b)\), and: \[F'(x) = f(x)\]

for all \(x \in (a,b)\).

Part II (Second Fundamental Theorem)

Let \(f\) be a continuous function on \([a,b]\). If \(F\) is any antiderivative of \(f\) on \([a,b]\) (i.e., \(F' = f\)), then:

\[\int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a)\]

Interpretation

Part I Interpretation

Part I tells us that every continuous function has an antiderivative, namely the integral function with variable upper limit. It also shows that differentiation undoes integration.

Part II Interpretation

Part II provides a powerful method for evaluating definite integrals: instead of computing limits of Riemann sums, we can find an antiderivative and evaluate it at the endpoints.

Proof Sketch

Proof of Part I

Let \(h > 0\) be small. By the definition of derivative:

\[F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{F(x+h) - F(x)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \int_x^{x+h} f(t) \, dt\]

By the Mean Value Theorem for integrals, there exists \(c \in [x, x+h]\) such that:

\[\int_x^{x+h} f(t) \, dt = f(c) \cdot h\]

Therefore: \[F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} f(c)\]

Since \(c \to x\) as \(h \to 0\) and \(f\) is continuous, we get \(F'(x) = f(x)\).

Proof of Part II

Let \(G(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt\) and let \(F\) be any antiderivative of \(f\).

By Part I, \(G'(x) = f(x) = F'(x)\), so \((F - G)'(x) = 0\) on \((a,b)\).

Therefore, \(F - G\) is constant, say \(F(x) - G(x) = C\).

Since \(G(a) = 0\), we have \(C = F(a)\).

Thus: \(F(x) - \int_a^x f(t) \, dt = F(a)\)

Setting \(x = b\): \(F(b) - \int_a^b f(t) \, dt = F(a)\)

Therefore: \(\int_a^b f(t) \, dt = F(b) - F(a)\)

Historical Development

The theorem was developed independently by: - Isaac Newton (1642-1727): Focused on the relationship between velocities and distances - Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646-1716): Developed the notation and formal framework - Isaac Barrow (1630-1677): Newton‚Äôs teacher, proved a geometric version - James Gregory (1638-1675): Published an early version with geometric focus

Consequences and Applications

1. Evaluation of Definite Integrals

Instead of computing limits of Riemann sums, we can use: \[\int_0^{\pi} \sin x \, dx = [-\cos x]_0^{\pi} = -\cos(\pi) + \cos(0) = 2\]

2. Existence of Antiderivatives

Every continuous function on an interval has an antiderivative.

3. Integration by Parts

Derived from the product rule and FTC: \[\int_a^b u \, dv = [uv]_a^b - \int_a^b v \, du\]

4. Change of Variables

The substitution rule follows from the chain rule and FTC: \[\int_a^b f(g(x))g'(x) \, dx = \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u) \, du\]

5. Physical Applications

  • Kinematics: Position is the integral of velocity
  • Work: Work is the integral of force over distance
  • Probability: Cumulative distribution functions are integrals of density functions

Relationship to Other Theorems

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is closely related to: - Mean Value Theorem: Used in the proof of Part I - Chain Rule: Generalizes to composition of functions - Taylor Series: Higher-order version using repeated integration

Examples

Example 1: Area Calculation

Find the area under \(f(x) = x^2\) from 0 to 2: \[\int_0^2 x^2 \, dx = \left[\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^2 = \frac{8}{3} - 0 = \frac{8}{3}\]

Example 2: Variable Upper Limit

If \(F(x) = \int_1^x \frac{1}{t} \, dt\), then by Part I: \[F'(x) = \frac{1}{x}\]

This shows that \(\ln x\) is an antiderivative of \(1/x\).

Example 3: Physics Application

A particle moves with velocity \(v(t) = 3t^2 - 2t\). The displacement from \(t = 0\) to \(t = 3\) is: \[s = \int_0^3 (3t^2 - 2t) \, dt = [t^3 - t^2]_0^3 = 27 - 9 = 18\]

Generalizations

  1. Lebesgue Integration: The theorem extends to more general integration theories
  2. Vector-Valued Functions: Applies component-wise to vector functions
  3. Complex Analysis: Cauchy‚Äôs theorem is an analog for complex functions
  4. Differential Forms: Stokes‚Äô theorem generalizes FTC to higher dimensions
